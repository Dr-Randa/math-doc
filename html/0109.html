On Additive Combinatorics of Permutations of ℤn.

[    [    [
Abstract

Let ℤn denote the ring of integers modulo n. In this paper we consider two extremal problems on permutations of ℤn, namely, the maximum size of a collection of permutations such that the sum of any two distinct permutations in the collection is again a permutation, and the maximum size of a collection of permutations such that the sum of any two distinct permutations in the collection is not a permutation. Let the sizes be denoted by s⁢(n) and t⁢(n) respectively. The case when n is even is trivial in both the cases, with s⁢(n)=1 and t⁢(n)=n!. For n odd, we prove s⁢(n)≥(n⁢ϕ⁢(n))/2k where k is the number of distinct prime divisors of n. When n is an odd prime we prove s⁢(n)≤e2π⁢n⁢((n-1)/e)n-12. For the second problem, we prove 2(n-1)/2.(n-12)!≤t⁢(n)≤2k.(n-1)!/ϕ⁢(n) when n is odd.

1 Introduction

For n∈ℤ, let ℤn denote the ring {0,…,n-1} with + and . as addition and multiplication modulo n respectively. Let 𝒮⁢(ℤn) denote the set of all permutations of the set ℤn. We are interested in obtaining bounds on the maximum size of a subset 𝒫 of 𝒮⁢(ℤn) in the case when two distinct permutations in 𝒫 sum up to a permutation (Section 3), and in the case when two distinct permutations in 𝒫 sum up to a non-permutation (Section 4). As far as we know the problems considered above are new, though a similar looking problem for difference of permutations is well studied, in the form of mutually orthogonal orthomorphisms of finite groups. For the sake of completeness, we discuss the connection between difference of permutations problem with the orthomorphisms problem in Section 5. The families of permutations we consider have similarities to reverse free and reverse full families of permutations as considered by Füredi et al. [] and Cibulka [].

2 Preliminaries

We review some well known facts and basic results from linear algebra, elementary number theory and combinatorics which will be used in later sections. A comprehensive reference for linear algebra notions discussed below is [].

Definition 2.1 (Vector Spaces, Bilinear Forms, Inner Products).

Let V be an n-dimensional vector space over the field F, which is naturally identified with the set Fn, with component-wise addition and scalar multiplication. A bilinear form on V is a function f:V×V→F which is linear in both arguments, i.e, f⁢(α⁢u+u′,β⁢v+v′)=α⁢β⁢f⁢(u,v)+α⁢f⁢(u,v′)+β⁢f⁢(u′,v)+f⁢(u′,v′) for u,u′,v,v′∈V and α,β∈F. A symmetric bilinear form is a bilinear form f with f⁢(u,v)=f⁢(v,u) for all u,v∈V. We will call a symmetric bilinear form on V as an inner product on V. The inner product on V given by ⟨u,v⟩=u1⁢v1+⋯+un⁢vn where, u=(u1,…,un) and v=(v1,…,vn) will be called the standard inner product on V.

Definition 2.2 (Totally isotropic subspaces).

Let V be an n-dimensional vector space with the standard inner product ⟨,⟩. We say that vectors u,v∈V are orthogonal, denoted by u⟂v, if ⟨u,v⟩=0. For a subspace S of V and a vector v∈V, we say v⟂S if v⟂u for all u∈S. For a subspace S of V, we define S⟂={v∈V:v⟂S} which is also a subspace of V. We call a subspace S of V totally isotropic if S⊆S⟂, or in other words, any two vectors in S are orthogonal.

We know the following fact from linear algebra.

Lemma 2.3.

Let V be an n-dimensional vector space with the standard inner product. Then for a subspace S of V, we have dim⁢(S)+dim⁢(S⟂)=n.

Next we discuss some basic notions from elementary number theory that will be used in the paper. An element s∈ℤn is said to be invertible if there exists t∈ℤn such that s⁢t=1 (recall that multiplication is in ℤn). The set of all invertible elements of ℤn is called the unit group of ℤn and is denoted by ℤn×. It is easily seen that ℤn× is a group under multiplication. We know that k∈ℤn is invertible if and only if gcd⁢(k,n)=1. The cardinality of the set {k∈ℤ:1≤k≤n-1,  gcd⁢(k,n)=1} is denoted by ϕ⁢(n), also known as Euler’s totient function in literature. The following results are well known.

Lemma 2.4.

Let n=∏i=1kpiαi, where p1,…,pk are distinct prime divisors of n. Then ϕ⁢(n)=∏i=1kpiαi-1⁢(pi-1).

Lemma 2.5 (Chinese Remainder Theorem).

Let n=∏i=1kpiαi, where p1,…,pk are distinct prime divisors of n. Then we have the following isomorphism,

ℤn ⟶ℤp1α1×ℤp2α2×⋯×ℤpkαk
s ⟼(s⁢ mod ⁢p1α1,…,s⁢ mod ⁢pkαk). (1)

From the above lemma we see that s=(s1,…,sk) is invertible in ℤn if and only if si is invertible in ℤpiαi for all i=1,…,k.

Finally we note the following combinatorial result.

Lemma 2.6.

Suppose the edges of the complete graph Kn, n≥2 are colored using k colors, such that the subgraph consisting of the edges of any given color is bipartite. Then n≤2k.

Proof.

Let V denote the vertex set of the complete graph Kn. We show an injection from V into the set {0,1}k. For each i, choose a partition {Ai,Bi} of V such that all edges of color i are between the sets Ai and Bi. By hypothesis, such a bi-partition exists for all i=1,…,k. For v∈V, define v~=(v1,…,vk) by,

vi={0 if v∈Ai,1 if v∈Bi.

We now show that the map v↦v~ is injective. Let v,w be two distinct vertices in V. Let i be the color of the edge v⁢w. Then we see that v~,w~ differ in the it⁢h component. This proves the injectivity of the map v↦v~ and the lemma follows. ∎

Notation 2.7.

We will denote permutations of ℤn as n-tuples (σ1,…,σn) where each σi∈ℤn and is distinct. This is not to be confused with the cycle representation of a permutation, as is customary in algebra. Since we do not use cycle representation of permutations in this paper, we hope there is no confusion. Let σ=(σ1,…,σn) and τ=(τ1,…,τn), be n-tuples over ℤn. Then σ±τ denotes the tuple (σ1±τ1,…,σn±τn). For c∈ℤn, c.σ will denote the tuple (c⁢σ1,…,c⁢σn), and c+σ will denote the tuple (c+σ1,…,c+σn).

Lemma 2.8.

Let n be even and (a1,…,an) and (b1,…,bn) be two permutations of ℤn. Then a+b is not a permutation of ℤn.

Proof.

Let c=a+b=(c1,…,cn). Treating a,b,c as n-tuples over ℤ we have, ci≡ai+bi (mod n). Summing up over all i, we have,

∑i=1nci ≡∑i=1n(ai+bi)⁢ (mod n)
or, ⁢∑i=1n(i-1) ≡∑i=1n2⁢(i-1)⁢ (mod n)
or, ⁢n⁢(n-1)2 ≡n(n-1)≡0 (mod n)

which is a contradiction as (n-1)/2 is not an integer when n is even. This proves the lemma. ∎

Lemma 2.9.

Let a=(0,…,an-1) and b=(b0,…,bn-1) be distinct permutations of the set {0,…,n-1} such that the component-wise sums ci=ai+bi are all distinct. Then there exist 0≤j,k≤n-1 such that cj=ck+1.

Proof.

Without loss of generality assume ci=ai+bi satisfy the ordering c0<c1<⋯<cn-1. Now suppose the claim is not true. Then we have, ci-ci-1≥2 for 1≤i≤n-1. Summing up we get 2⁢n-2≤∑i=1n-1(ci-ci-1)=cn-1-c0≤2⁢n-2. Thus ci-ci-1=2 for all 1≤i≤n-1, which implies ci=2⁢i for all 0≤i≤n-1. Now a0+b0=c0=0 implies a0=b0=0. Now c1=a1+b1=2, therefore we must have a1=b1=1. Continuing this way, we conclude ai=bi=i for all 0≤i≤n-1, contradicting the fact that the permutations were distinct. ∎

3 Sum of Permutations being Permutation

In this section, we consider the maximum size of a collection of permutations of ℤn such that sum of any two distinct permutations in the collection is again a permutation (not necessarily in the collection). A collection 𝒫 of permutations of ℤn is said to satisfy property (𝐏𝟏) if for any two distinct permutations σ,τ in 𝒫, σ+τ is again a permutation of ℤn. Let s⁢(n)=max⁡{|𝒫|:𝒫⊆𝒮⁢(ℤn),𝒫⁢ satisfies ⁢(𝐏𝟏)}.

From Lemma 2.8, we note that s⁢(n)=1 when n is even. Now we present a construction for the lower bound on s⁢(n) when n is odd.

Lemma 3.1.

Let n be an odd number ≥3. Then s⁢(n)≥(n⁢ϕ⁢(n))/2k where ϕ⁢(n) is the Euler’s totient function and k is the number of distinct prime divisors of n.

Proof.

Our construction is based on the following observations.

  1. (a)

    For a permutation τ of ℤn, k.τ is a permutation of ℤn if and only if k is invertible in ℤn.

  2. (b)

    For a permutation τ of ℤn, k+τ is a permutation for all k∈ℤn.

  3. (c)

    Let n=∏i=1kpiαi, where pi’s are distinct prime divisors of n. Then there exists a subset S of invertible elements of ℤn with |S|=∏i=1kpαi-1⁢(pi-1)/2 such that for any x,y∈S, x+y is invertible in ℤn. To describe the set S, we use the isomorphism in Lemma 2.5. Let S={s∈ℤn:s=(s1,…,sk)⁢ where ⁢si≡ci⁢ mod ⁢pi⁢ for some ⁢1≤ci≤(pi-1)/2}. Note that in the description of S, each si has piαi-1⁢(pi-1)/2 choices, and hence the set S has the desired cardinality. Further each element of S is invertible in ℤn. Now for s,t∈S, we have s+t=(s1+t1,…,sk+tk) where s=(s1,…,sk) and t=(t1,…,tk). By definition of S, observe that si+ti≢0 (mod pi) for all 1≤i≤k. Thus s+t is invertible in ℤn.

Now consider the set 𝒫={s.(x+0,x+1,…,x+n-1):s∈S,x∈ℤn}. By observations (a),(b) and (c), we see that 𝒫 consists of permutations of ℤn. Let σ,τ be two distinct permutations in 𝒫 with σ=s.(x+0,…,x+n-1) and τ=t.(y+0,…,y+n-1). Then σ+τ=(s⁢x+t⁢y)+(s+t).(0,1,…,n-1). Since s+t is invertible, by observations (a) and (b), we conclude that σ+τ is a permutation. Thus 𝒫 satisfies (𝐏𝟏). Finally we observe that |𝒫|=n.|S|=n.∏i=1kpiαi-1(pi-1)/2=(n.ϕ(n))/2k. This proves the lemma. ∎

Remark 3.2.

We note that when n is a prime number, the bound in Lemma 3.1 reduces to n⁢(n-1)/2.

We now establish an upper bound on s⁢(n) in the case when n is an odd prime.

Theorem 3.3 (Cauchy-Davenport).

Let n be any prime number and let A,B⊂ℤn. Then |A+B|≥min⁡{n,|A|+|B|-1}, where A+B={a+b:a∈A,b∈B}.

Lemma 3.4.

Let n be an odd prime. Then s⁢(n)≤e2π⁢n⁢(n-1e)n-12.

Proof.

We prove the case n=3 seperately. It suffices to prove s⁢(3)≤3. This follows from the observation that among any four permutations σ1,σ2,σ3,σ4 of ℤ3, there exist permutations σi,σj such that σj is obtained from σi by interchanging exactly one pair, and thus σi+σj is not a permutation.

From now on we assume n>3. Let 𝒫 with |𝒫|=m be a collection of permutations of ℤn satisfying (𝐏𝟏). Let Ai=(ai⁢1,…,ai⁢n) be the permutations in 𝒫 for i=1,…,m. Let M denote the m×n matrix with (i,j)t⁢h entry ai⁢j. When n is prime the ring ℤn is infact a field, and thus we can regard the permutations Ai as vectors in n-dimensional vector space (ℤn)n. Let ⟨,⟩ be the standard inner product on (ℤn)n. Then, we note that for a permutation A of ℤn we have,

⟨A,A⟩=∑i=0n-1i2=n6⁢(n-1)⁢(2⁢n-1)≡0 (mod ⁢n). (2)

Claim: rank⁢(M)≤(n-1)/2. Let u,v be vectors corresponding to two different rows in M. Then from (2), we have ⟨u,u⟩=⟨v,v⟩=0. Further since u+v is also a permutation, we have ⟨u+v,u+v⟩=0. Using these relations, we conclude ⟨u,v⟩=0 for all u,v∈M. Let S be the subspace spanned by the rows of M. Then, by earlier observation S is totally isotropic, i.e, S⊆S⟂. Combining this with the relation dim⁢(S)+dim⁢(S⟂)=n, we have rank⁢(M)=dim⁢(S)≤⌊n/2⌋=(n-1)/2 as n is odd.

Let r=rank⁢(M). We may assume without loss of generality, by permuting the columns of M if necessary, that the first r columns of M are linearly independent. Thus there exist linear functions fi:(ℤn)r→ℤn for i=1,…,n-r such that a row of M can be written as (x1,x2,…,xr,y1,y2,…,yn-r) where yi=fi⁢(x1,…,xr). Thus any two rows in M have different projection on the first r positions.

Claim: For any odd number k∈{1,r}, let M′ be an l×n submatrix of M in which all the rows agree on the first k positions. Then at most (n-k)/2 distinct elements of ℤn appear in any column of M′ beyond k. Suppose, for the sake of contradiction, that the j-th column of M′ for some j>k contains more than (n-k)/2 elements of ℤn. Let B be the set of elements that appear in the j-th column of M′. By Theorem 3.3, |B+B|≥n-k+1. Hence B+B contains some 2⁢x where x is the common entry in some i-th column, i≤k, of M′. Thus either x∈B, which violates the property that every row of M′ is a permutation or ∃x′,x′′∈B, x′≠x′′ such that x′+x′′=2⁢x which violates the property that sum of any two rows of M′ is a permutation.

Since M is completely determined by its first r rows and the above claim, the total number of rows m of M is at most n×∏i=2r⌈(n-i+1)/2⌉. Hence m≤n⁢(∏j=0⌊r/2⌋-1((n-1)/2-j))2 and since r≤(n-1)/2, we get m≤n⁢(((n-1)/2)!(⌈(n-1)/4⌉)!)2 which by Stirling’s approximation gives us m≤e2π⁢n⁢(n-1e)n-12. ∎

4 Sum of Permutations being Non-Permutation

We consider another variant of the previous problem. We now seek a collection of permutations of ℤn such that any two distinct permutations sum up to a non-permutation. We say that a collection 𝒫 of permutations of ℤn satisfies property (𝐏𝟐) if any two distinct permutations in 𝒫 sum up to a non-permutation. Let t⁢(n)=max⁡{|𝒫|:𝒫⊆𝒮⁢(ℤn),𝒫⁢ satisfies ⁢(𝐏𝟐)}. From Lemma 2.8, we see that t⁢(n)=n! when n is even. We consider the case when n is odd in the following lemma.

Lemma 4.1.

Let n be an odd number. Then 2(n-1)/2.(n-12)!≤t⁢(n)≤2k⁢(n-1)!/ϕ⁢(n), where k is the number of distinct prime divisors of n.

Proof.

We prove t⁢(n)≥2(n-1)/2.(n-12)!. We say the pair of permutations (a0,…,an-1) and (b0,…,bn-1) of the set {0,…,n-1} are admissible if the sums ai+bi, 0≤i≤m-1, are not all distinct (the addition being in ℤ). We construct a collection 𝒫 of mutually admissible permutations of {0,…,n-1}. Note that 𝒫 when viewed as a set of permutations of ℤn, satisfies (𝐏𝟐). Let m=(n-1)/2. For 0≤i≤m-1, let Bi=(2⁢i,2⁢i+1) and B¯i=(2⁢i+1,2⁢i). Let c=c0⁢c1⁢…⁢cm-1 be a binary string of length m, and σ be a permutation of {0,…,m-1}. Define, Pσ,c=(x0,x1,…,xn-1) such that,

(x2⁢i,x2⁢i+1)={Bσ⁢(i) if ⁢ci=0,B¯σ⁢(i) if ⁢c1=1

for all i=0,…,m-1. It is easily observed that Pσ,c is a permutation of the set {0,…,n-1} and Pσ,c≠Pσ′,c′ for (σ,c)≠(σ′,c′). Let 𝒫 denote the collection of permutations {Pσ,c}. We now show that any two permutations in the collection 𝒫 are admissible. Let Pσ,c and Pσ′,c′ be two distinct permutations in 𝒫. We consider two cases:

Case: c≠c′. Let i be such that ci≠ci′. Without loss of generality assume ci=0,ci′=1. Let Pσ,c=(x0,…,xn-1) and Pσ′,c′=(x0′,…,xn-1′). Then we have, (x2⁢i,x2⁢i+1)=Bσ⁢(i)=(a,a+1) and (x2⁢i′,x2⁢i+1′)=B¯σ′⁢(i)=(b+1,b) for some a,b. Thus x2⁢i+x2⁢i′=x2⁢i+1+x2⁢i+1′=a+b+1, and hence the permutations are admissible.

Case: c=c′. In this case we must have σ≠σ′. In the block Bi=(2⁢i,2⁢i+1), let us call 2⁢i as the little end and 2⁢i+1 as the big end. Then c=c′ implies that in the component-wise addition of Pσ,c and Pσ′,c′ the little ends are summed with little ends, and big ends are summed with big ends. Let L be the set of sums of little ends, i.e, L={2⁢σ⁢(i)+2⁢σ′⁢(i):0≤i≤m-1} and B be the sums of big ends, i.e., B={2⁢σ⁢(i)+2⁢σ′⁢(i)+2:0≤i≤m-1}. We show that L∩B is non-empty which will prove that Pσ,c and Pσ′,c′ are admissible. By Lemma 2.9, there exist 0≤j,k≤m-1 such that σ⁢(j)+σ′⁢(j)=σ⁢(k)+σ′⁢(k)+1, or 2⁢σ⁢(j)+2⁢σ′⁢(j)=2⁢σ⁢(k)+2⁢σ′⁢(k)+2. We see that the left side of the identity is in L and the right side is in B. Thus L∩B is non-empty.

Hence the collection of permutations Pσ,c, when viewed as permutations of ℤn satisfy (𝐏𝟐). Finally we note that the number of permutations is 2m.m!=2(n-1)/2.(n-12)!.

Next we prove t⁢(n)≤2k⁢(n-1)!/ϕ⁢(n). Let n=∏i=1kpiαi. We define a relation ∼ on the set of permutations of ℤn by σ∼τ if σ=t+s.τ for some t∈ℤn and s∈ℤn× (recall that ℤn× denotes the set of invertible elements of ℤn). We show that ∼ is an equivalence relation on 𝒮⁢(ℤn). Note that σ∼σ as σ=0+1.⁢σ. To show symmetry, assume σ∼τ. Then σ=t+s.τ for some t∈ℤn and s∈ℤn×. Rearranging we have, τ=-t+s-1.σ. Thus τ∼σ. To show transitivity assume σ∼τ and τ∼γ. Then we have, σ=t1+s1.τ and τ=t2+s2.γ for some t1,t2∈ℤn and s1,s2∈ℤn×. From the previous relations we see that σ=(t1+s1⁢t2)+s1⁢s2.γ, and therefore σ∼γ. Thus ∼ is an equivalence relation on 𝒮⁢(ℤn). We now make the following claim:

Claim: Let 𝒫 be a collection of permutations of ℤn satisfying (𝐏𝟐), and ∼ be the equivalence relation on 𝒮⁢(ℤn) as defined. Then each equivalence class of ∼ contains atmost 2k permutations from 𝒫.

Let σ1,…,σm be permutations from 𝒫 which belong to the same equivalence class. Let σ be a permutation such that σi∼σ for all i=1,…,m. Let si,ti be such that σi=ti+si.σ. Now σi+σj=(ti+tj)+(si+sj).σ. From observations (a) and (b) in the proof of Lemma 3.1, we see that σi+σj is a non-permutation if and only if si+sj is not invertible in ℤn. Thus pr|(si+sj) for some r, 1≤r≤k. Consider the edge coloring of the complete graph Km on vertex set {1,…,m} using k colors as follows. We color the edge i⁢j with the color min{r∈{1,…,k}:pr|(si+sj)}.

Subclaim: For each color i∈{1,…,k}, the edges of color i span a bipartite subgraph of Km. We prove this by showing there are no monochromatic odd cycles. Assume v1⁢v2⁢…⁢vl is a monochromatic odd cycle. Let i be the color of the edges in the cycle. Then pi|(s1+s2),pi|(s2+s3),…,pi|(sl-1+sl),pi|(sl+s1). Adding the above we get pi|2(s1+⋯+sl) or pi|(s1+⋯+sl) as pi is odd. Now, from the (l-1)/2 relations pi|(s1+s2),pi|(s3+s4),…,pi|(sl-2+sl-1) we get pi|(s1+⋯+sl-1). Combining this with pi|(s1+⋯+sl) we get pi|sl which contradicts the fact that sl in invertible in ℤn. Thus there are no monochromatic odd cycles, and hence edges of a fixed color span a bipartite subgraph of Km.

The subclaim allows us to apply Lemma 2.6 to the coloring of Km, and hence we conclude that m≤2k. This proves the claim.

Finally we notice that each equivalence class of ∼ contains n⁢ϕ⁢(n) elements, and hence there are n!/(n⁢ϕ⁢(n))=(n-1)!/ϕ⁢(n) equivalence classes. Now from the claim, each equivalence class contains at most 2k elements from 𝒫, and hence |𝒫|≤2k⁢(n-1)!/ϕ⁢(n). ∎

Remark 4.2.

We note that for a prime number n, the upper bound ∼(n/e)n⁢n whereas the lower bound ∼(n/e)n/2⁢n. Thus there is a quadratic gap between the upper and lower bounds. We also mention that one way to obtain permutations summing up to a non permutation is to consider a family of permutations with mutual reverse. Two permutations σ,τ of {0,…,n-1} are said to have a mutual reverse if there exist i≠j such that σ⁢(i)=τ⁢(j) and σ⁢(j)=τ⁢(i). We call such a family of permutations as reverse full. Note that a reverse full family of permutations satisfies (𝐏𝟐). From a result of Cibulka [] on size of reverse-free families of permutations, the maximum size of a reverse full family of permutations on n symbols is at most nn/2+O⁢(log⁡n), though we are unaware of any non-trivial lower bound for the same. Our construction achieves ∼(n/e)n/2⁢n, but under a much more flexbile constraint.

5 Differences of Permutations being Permutation

In this section, we wish to obtain upper and lower bounds on the maximum size of set 𝒫⊆𝒮⁢(ℤn) such that for any two distinct permutations σ,τ∈𝒫, σ-τ is also a permutation of ℤn. We say that 𝒫⊆𝒮⁢(ℤn) satisfies property (𝐏𝟑) if for any two σ,τ∈𝒫, σ≠τ, σ-τ is a permutation of ℤn. Let f⁢(n)=max⁡{|𝒫|:𝒫⊆𝒮⁢(ℤn)⁢ satisfies ⁢(𝐏𝟑)}.

Question 5.1.

What is a good estimate for f⁢(n) as defined above ?

The above question is only superficially different from the well studied problem of finding mutually orthogonal orthomorphisms of finite groups (See []). For a finite group G, a bijection θ:G→G is called an orthomorphism of G if the map x↦θ⁢(x)-x is a bijection. Two orthomorphisms θ,ϕ are called orthogonal if θ-ϕ is a bijection. It is not hard to see that a set of k permutations of ℤn satisfying (P3) gives a set of k-1 mutually orthogonal orthomorphisms of ℤn and vice versa. Orthogonal orthomorphisms have been used in construction of mutually orthogonal latin squares (MOLS). If we write the permutations of 𝒫 as rows of a matrix, we get a |𝒫|×n matrix over ℤn with the property that the difference of any two distinct rows is a permutation of ℤn. We point out that such matrices have been used in connection with constructions of Latin Squares and Orthogonal Arrays (cf. [, Chapter 22]). Well known bounds for mutually orthogonal orthomorphisms yield the following lemma.

Lemma 5.2.

For n≥2, we have,

  1. (a)

    f⁢(n)≤n-1 ,

  2. (b)

    f⁢(n)=1 , when n is an even number,

  3. (c)

    f⁢(n)=n-1 , when n is a prime number.

Determine f⁢(n) for odd composite number n.
